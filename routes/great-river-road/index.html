Great River Road Itinerary: 10 Days from the Headwaters to New Orleans

The Great River Road follows the Mississippi River for roughly 2,000 miles through 10 states — from Itasca State Park in Minnesota, where the river begins, to New Orleans, Louisiana, where it reaches the Gulf. You can drive the whole route in 10 days at a steady pace, do it in 7 if you push, or take 14 to linger. Most people split it: a northern segment (Minnesota to St. Louis) that peaks in late September and October for fall color and bluff-country river views, and a southern segment (Memphis to New Orleans) that is best from November through March, when the Deep South is cool and the blues clubs are full. This guide covers the exact route, daily mileage, the stops worth your time, and the ones you can skip. What Is the Great River Road?

The Great River Road is not a single highway — it is a network of state and local roads that hug both banks of the Mississippi River, strung together and signed as one National Scenic Byway. It runs the length of the river through 10 states, and because it follows the water rather than the interstate, it threads through river towns, past locks and dams, along limestone bluffs, and out across the flat Mississippi Delta. You navigate it by watching for the green pilot's-wheel signs — a steamboat's wheel on a green shield — which mark the route on both sides of the river.

Because it's a network, you're often choosing a bank. In the north, the Wisconsin and Minnesota sides trade the best views back and forth; in the south, the Mississippi and Louisiana sides each have their own towns and history. This itinerary picks the more scenic or more interesting bank at each stretch and tells you when to cross.

When to Go: North in Fall, South in Winter

The Great River Road runs 1,500 miles of latitude, so no single month is best for the whole thing. The trick is to match the season to the section.

Northern section (Minnesota → St. Louis) — late September to late October. This is bluff country, and it turns gold and red in autumn. Lake Pepin, the Great River Bluffs, and the Galena hills are at their best when the maples change, usually the first three weeks of October. Summer works too, but fall is the reason to drive the north.

Southern section (Memphis → New Orleans) — November to March. The Delta and the Gulf coast are hot and humid from May through September. Come in the cool months and you get comfortable walking weather in Natchez and New Orleans, thinner crowds, and the peak of the live-music calendar leading into Mardi Gras.

If you're driving the whole route in one push, aim for mid-October: you'll catch the tail of northern color and reach the Deep South as it cools off.

Planning the Drive: Direction, Rentals & Road Conditions

Which direction? The classic way is north to south, following the river's flow from the trickle at Lake Itasca to the mile-wide river at New Orleans — the trip builds naturally from quiet headwaters to the crescendo of the French Quarter. Driving south to north works too and is a little cheaper on one-way car rentals (you end near a major hub), but you lose that sense of the river growing beside you.

One-way or loop? Because the route is a straight shot rather than a circle, most people fly into Minneapolis, drive south, and fly home from New Orleans — a one-way rental. Expect a drop-off fee (often $150–$400) for leaving the car in a different city; factor it into the budget above, or build a return leg on the interstate if you'd rather avoid it. If you only have time for half, the northern and southern segments each start and end at a good airport (Minneapolis/St. Louis, and Memphis/New Orleans).

What are the roads like? This is easy driving — paved two-lane and small highways the whole way, no mountain passes. The northern bluff roads wind and climb a little; the southern Delta is dead flat and straight. Gas and cell coverage are reliable in the towns but thin on the rural stretches between them, especially in the Delta between Memphis and Vicksburg, so top up when you can. A few riverside spots use seasonal ferries rather than bridges — check the schedule before you count on a crossing.

How long is each half? Roughly 1,000 miles and five days per segment. Split at St. Louis (where Route 66 crosses) or at Memphis if you want the whole Deep South in one trip.

The 10-Day Itinerary, Day by Day

Daily mileages below are planning estimates for the scenic route (not the interstate), so build in extra time for overlooks and river-town main streets. Every day pairs 2–3 highlights with a place to eat or stay and one honest "skip this."

Day 1

Itasca State Park to Minneapolis

200 miles · ~4 hours driving

Highlights: Start where the river does — at Lake Itasca, walk across the line of stepping stones at the Mississippi Headwaters, narrow enough to hop the river on foot. Then drive south through Minnesota's pine-and-lake country. Stop at Charles A. Lindbergh State Park near Little Falls if you want to stretch your legs.

Eat / stay: Overnight in Minneapolis; dinner in the North Loop, then walk the Stone Arch Bridge for your first big-city river view.

Skip: The "world's largest" roadside statues along US-71 are a two-minute photo stop at most — don't plan the day around them.
Day 2

Minneapolis to La Crosse via Red Wing & Winona

160 miles · ~3.5 hours driving

Highlights: The bluffs begin. Red Wing for the pottery, the Barn Bluff climb, and the historic riverfront; Lake Pepin, the widest natural stretch of the Mississippi; and the overlooks around Winona. This is arguably the prettiest driving day of the entire route.

Eat / stay: Lunch in Red Wing; overnight in La Crosse, Wisconsin. Drive up to Grandad Bluff at sunset for a three-state view.

Skip: Don't detour inland to save time. The whole point today is staying on the river road (Minnesota 61 / Wisconsin 35) for the water and the cliffs.
Day 3

La Crosse to Dubuque & Galena

110 miles · ~2.5 hours driving

Highlights: Cross into Iowa for Effigy Mounds National Monument, where ancient peoples built animal-shaped earthworks on the bluffs above the river. Take in the view from Pikes Peak State Park, then finish in Galena, Illinois — Ulysses S. Grant's town, with a preserved 19th-century main street.

Eat / stay: Overnight in Galena; dinner on Main Street. If you'd rather stay riverside, Dubuque is 20 minutes west.

Skip: The riverboat casinos in Dubuque unless gambling is your thing — you'll get more out of Galena's old town.
Day 4

Dubuque to the Quad Cities

90 miles · ~2 hours driving

Highlights: A shorter, easy day. Hike the river overlooks at Mississippi Palisades State Park, then watch barges lock through at Lock & Dam 15 in the Quad Cities — the visitor area lets you see the river's engineering up close. Art lovers should stop at the Figge Art Museum in Davenport.

Eat / stay: Overnight in the Quad Cities (Davenport, Bettendorf, Rock Island, or Moline).

Skip: Over-scheduling. This is a built-in breather between the northern bluffs and the run to Mark Twain country — use the spare hours.
Day 5

Quad Cities to Hannibal

150 miles · ~3 hours driving

Highlights: Hannibal, Missouri is the boyhood home of Samuel Clemens — Mark Twain — and it leans into it well. Tour the Mark Twain Boyhood Home & Museum, go underground at Mark Twain Cave (the one from Tom Sawyer), and take a paddle-wheeler cruise on the river.

Eat / stay: Overnight in Hannibal's historic district so you can walk the old streets after the day-trippers leave.

Skip: The kitschier Twain-themed gift shops. The cave and the museum are the real thing; the T-shirt stores aren't.
Day 6

Hannibal to St. Louis

120 miles · ~2.5 hours driving

Highlights: Gateway Arch National Park — ride the tram to the top for the river-and-city view, and see the museum on Westward Expansion beneath it. On the way in, detour to the Old Chain of Rocks Bridge, the former Route 66 crossing (the two great American road trips meet here).

Eat / stay: Overnight in St. Louis; try toasted ravioli in The Hill or St. Louis-style barbecue.

Skip: Turning up at the Arch tram mid-afternoon without a ticket — book a morning slot to avoid the lines.
Day 7

St. Louis to Memphis via New Madrid

285 miles · ~5 hours driving

Highlights: The longest driving day, and the transition into the South. Break it up at Trail of Tears State Park and at New Madrid, Missouri, epicenter of the enormous 1811–12 earthquakes, where a riverside observation deck explains what happened. By evening you cross into the Delta at Memphis.

Eat / stay: Overnight in Memphis; ribs near Beale Street, and save the National Civil Rights Museum for the morning.

Skip: Trying to see everything today. This is a mileage day — pick one or two stops and keep moving.
Day 8

Memphis to Clarksdale & Vicksburg

240 miles · ~4.5 hours driving

Highlights: This is the blues day. Clarksdale, Mississippi sits at the legendary Crossroads of Highways 61 and 49; visit the Delta Blues Museum and catch live music at a juke joint like Ground Zero. Then cross the flat, wide-open Delta to Vicksburg and its sprawling National Military Park.

Eat / stay: Lunch and museum in Clarksdale; overnight in Vicksburg above the river.

Skip: Rushing the Delta after dark. Arrive in Clarksdale with daylight left for the museum and the Crossroads.
Day 9

Vicksburg to Natchez & Baton Rouge

170 miles · ~3.5 hours driving

Highlights: Natchez, Mississippi is the oldest town on the river and one of the prettiest — bluff-top views, antebellum architecture, and the northern end of the Natchez Trace. Cross the river bridge and continue into Louisiana toward the state capital.

Eat / stay: Lunch on the bluff in Natchez; overnight in Baton Rouge.

Skip: Backtracking onto the Natchez Trace Parkway unless you have a spare day — it's beautiful but heads away from the river.
Day 10

Baton Rouge to New Orleans via the River Road plantations

110 miles · ~2.5 hours driving (allow extra for a plantation tour)

Highlights: The final stretch runs the River Road plantation corridor between Baton Rouge and New Orleans. For an honest telling of this landscape's history, tour the Whitney Plantation, the one museum here built to center the lives of the enslaved people who worked it. Then arrive in New Orleans: the French Quarter, the river walk at Woldenberg Park, and beignets at Café du Monde.

Eat / stay: Finish in New Orleans — dinner in the Marigny or a po'boy in the Quarter, and live music to close out the drive.

Skip: The house-and-gardens plantation tours that gloss over slavery. Choose Whitney for the history that actually happened here.

15 Best Stops on the Great River Road

If you can't do the full drive, these are the stops worth building a trip around, north to south.

  1. Mississippi Headwaters, Lake Itasca (MN). Where the river begins — a shallow, clear outlet you can literally walk across. The symbolic start of the whole route.
  2. Red Wing (MN). Bluffs, pottery, and a walkable historic riverfront. Climb Barn Bluff for the view down Lake Pepin.
  3. Lake Pepin (MN/WI). The river's widest natural lake, ringed by bluffs. The prettiest slow-driving stretch in the north.
  4. Effigy Mounds National Monument (IA). Ancient animal-shaped earthworks on the bluffs — a moving, uncrowded stop with big river views.
  5. Galena (IL). A preserved 19th-century town and Ulysses S. Grant's home, tucked into the hills just off the river.
  6. Mississippi Palisades State Park (IL). Cliffs, hiking, and overlooks near the Iowa border — an easy leg-stretch with a payoff.
  7. Lock & Dam 15, Quad Cities (IA/IL). Watch barges rise and fall through the locks; the best place to understand how the river is engineered.
  8. Hannibal (MO). Mark Twain's boyhood town — the museum, the cave from Tom Sawyer, and a paddle-wheeler on the river.
  9. Gateway Arch National Park (MO). Ride to the top of the Arch for the classic river-and-city panorama; the underground museum is excellent.
  10. New Madrid (MO). Epicenter of the 1811–12 earthquakes that briefly ran the Mississippi backward; a quiet riverside stop with a good story.
  11. Memphis (TN). Beale Street music, world-class barbecue, and the National Civil Rights Museum at the Lorraine Motel — a must.
  12. Clarksdale (MS). The Crossroads of the blues: the Delta Blues Museum, juke joints, and Highway 61 running dead straight through the cotton.
  13. Vicksburg National Military Park (MS). A vast, walkable Civil War battlefield on the bluffs above the river.
  14. Natchez (MS). The oldest town on the river — bluff-top overlooks, historic architecture, and the head of the Natchez Trace.
  15. Whitney Plantation & the River Road (LA). The plantation museum that centers the history of slavery, on the road into New Orleans.

Northern vs. Southern Section: Which Is Better?

Short answer: drive the north for scenery and the south for culture and food. If you can only do one half, let the season decide — the north in fall, the south in winter.

Practical tips

  • Follow the pilot's wheel, not just your GPS. Navigation apps default to the interstate; the green signs keep you on the scenic river route.
  • Pick your bank. In the north, the Wisconsin and Minnesota sides trade the best views — cross at the bridges. In the south, the east bank has most of the towns.
  • Book the big-ticket stops ahead. The Arch tram, Whitney Plantation tours, and fall weekends in Galena and Red Wing sell out.
  • Drive north-to-south. Going with the river's flow builds the trip from quiet headwaters to the crescendo of New Orleans.
  • Driving an EV? Fast charging is easy in the cities; plan a buffer through the rural northern bluffs and the Delta between Memphis and Vicksburg.

Frequently Asked Questions

How many days do you need to drive the Great River Road?

Plan for 10 days to drive the full 2,000-mile route comfortably. You can do it in 7 at a fast pace, or split it into a 5-day northern segment (Minnesota to St. Louis) and a 5-day southern segment (St. Louis to New Orleans). Two weeks lets you slow down for state parks, blues clubs, and river towns.

What states does the Great River Road go through?

Ten, following the river north to south: Minnesota, Wisconsin, Iowa, Illinois, Missouri, Kentucky, Tennessee, Arkansas, Mississippi, and Louisiana.

Is the Great River Road worth driving?

Yes — if you like variety over a single dramatic landscape. The northern bluff country between Red Wing and Dubuque rivals any river drive in the country, and the southern Delta from Memphis to New Orleans is the heart of American blues and food history. It's also less crowded than the coastal highways and cheap to drive.

What is the best part of the Great River Road?

For scenery, the northern stretch from Red Wing to Dubuque — bluffs, Lake Pepin, and river overlooks. For culture and food, the southern stretch from Memphis through Clarksdale, Vicksburg, and Natchez to New Orleans. If you can only drive one half, pick the north in fall or the south in winter.

Great River Road vs Route 66: which should you drive?

Route 66 is a west-bound nostalgia drive across the desert Southwest; the Great River Road is a north-south river drive through green bluff country and the Deep South. Choose Route 66 for retro Americana and open landscapes, the Great River Road for water views, small river towns, blues, and Southern food. Fittingly, the two cross in St. Louis.

Can you drive the Great River Road in an EV?

Yes. Fast charging is easy along the cities of the route — Minneapolis, the Quad Cities, St. Louis, Memphis, Baton Rouge, and New Orleans all have DC fast chargers. The gaps to plan for are the rural northern bluffs and the Delta between Memphis and Vicksburg: arrive with a comfortable buffer and charge in town rather than counting on roadside stalls.
